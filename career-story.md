# Career Story — Christiaan Verhoef

*Deep biographical material for cover letter writing and interview prep. Raw proof points, character context, origin stories.*

---

## KONGSBERG — The Origin Story (2012–2013)

### Context

Two separate assignments. Technically still an intern, but applied for and got a full field service engineer position. The role was all-in-one: consulting, documentation, architecture, hardware installation, data translation, strict procedure adherence — deployed to oil rigs and remote offices in highly regulated environments. No internet, no ability to call in support. You were the support.

Deployed to: Jordan (BP), Oman (BP), Houston Texas (BP), Azerbaijan (BP/GeoServices).

Was the only intern at the site, alongside four experienced engineers. Was also the only one who could do all parts of the job — software, hardware, data negotiation, strict procedure compliance — end to end.

### The Training Observation

Kongsberg's training model: three weeks of PowerPoint presentations (classical education), followed by three months of unstructured experimentation ("do what you want, explore the system"), then field deployment with a senior engineer.

The other engineers used the three-month period to stick to procedure and do the minimum. Christiaan used it to run experiments, push the system, learn what broke. This difference showed in the field: the others could execute known procedures. They could not navigate the unknown.

This observation became the seed of everything that followed — the thesis, the Beer Game, the living labs, the entire learning philosophy.

### Jordan — The Dead-Switch Problem

First major deployment: BP Jordan, with senior engineer Stian Skeavesland. Assignment: install a KONGSBERG system at the BP office and at the rig.

At the office, following the deployment procedure to the letter led to a failure: the database would create, respond, but crash as soon as the full data injection began. The local IT administrator was adamant it was not his hardware, not his network, not his problem.

Day three. Almost behind schedule. Christiaan deployed a dead-switch in the infrastructure room. The database deployed cleanly. Proven: a networking issue on the client side, not the KONGSBERG system.

The administrator's certainty was wrong. The procedure did not anticipate the problem. The engineer who had only learned procedure would have been stuck.

**Proof point:** Technical problem-solving under time pressure, in a highly regulated environment, with no remote support, when the official procedure fails and the client insists they are not the problem.

### Houston — The Underutilization

Houston was the opposite problem. The assignment was minimal: drive to the rig, plug in a laptop, confirm internet connectivity. That was it.

Christiaan recognized this was a box-ticking exercise. The skills were not being used. When an urgent deployment came up for Azerbaijan, he trained a local Houston engineer to cover the remaining responsibilities and went to Azerbaijan instead.

(The Houston engineer — who had described himself as highly skilled with a military background and strong mathematics — managed to fail at the task. This detail has never stopped being relevant to conversations about training methodology.)

### Azerbaijan — The Contract Confrontation

Azerbaijan assignment: maintain an existing BP rig deployment while it was being upgraded by another engineer.

GeoServices (a subcontractor on the rig) was not sharing data with KONGSBERG. GeoServices' reason: the data has commercial value to them. The actual situation: BP owned that data by contract, and KONGSBERG needed it to do its job.

Christiaan spoke with the GeoServices engineers. No result. Went to their office in person. Still no result. The engineers were pleasant. Nothing moved.

He picked up the BP contract — approximately the size of a large bible — found the relevant clauses, and went directly to the GeoServices division manager. The manager was visibly surprised. Christiaan cited the specific contract clauses. The data was being withheld in violation of a BP contractual right. KONGSBERG needed it. This needed to be resolved.

Within one day, the data was flowing.

**Proof point:** Commercial/contractual dispute resolution. No seniority, no title, no company backing beyond a contract. Read the document, found the clause, held the position.

### The Thesis — And Why It Was Never Delivered

One year after returning from the field, Christiaan proposed a thesis for KONGSBERG on learning methodology. The premise: instead of three weeks of PowerPoint followed of three months of free experimentation, engineers should face simulated problems — realistic scenarios designed to trigger the same failures and decisions they would encounter in the field.

This is the Conscious Competence model applied to technical training. You learn what you don't know by encountering it in a controlled environment, not by reading about it.

The thesis was completed. It was never delivered to KONGSBERG. Christiaan was embarrassed — convinced the work was not good enough, unsure of its quality. He struggles when uncertain about his own output. The thesis sat. KONGSBERG never got the final version.

He declined the permanent contract offer at the same time. Was still in school, was confused by the terms, and chose to continue his studies. In hindsight: one of the clearest examples of the gap between what he could do and what he was willing to claim credit for.

**Character note:** High performer who underestimates his own output when uncertain. Confident in the field, hesitant to own the product afterward. This has shown up more than once.

### What Kongsberg Actually Taught

The field experience confirmed what the training observation suggested: classical instruction followed by unstructured experimentation does not build resilience. It builds procedure-followers.

Simulation-based learning — placing people inside realistic problems before the stakes are real — builds the pattern recognition that lets them act when the procedure fails.

This became the foundation for:
- The Beer Game redesign at Windesheim (supply chain simulation)
- The Spark! Living Lab (student experiments in real conditions)
- The Aeres course integration (eight courses merged into one sprint-based simulation track)
- The KnopenKoning decision-making simulation
- The COVID-19 Mega Project (pandemic scenario learning)
- The GitLab cover letter framing ("the gap between current reality and the new method became visible, felt rather than argued")

Every major learning design decision since 2013 traces back to what he saw happen — and not happen — in those three months at KONGSBERG.

## The Real Insight — Procedure AND Initiative, Simultaneously

Most people are one or the other: they follow process, or they innovate. They struggle to do both at the same time. Regulated environments tend to attract procedure-followers. Creative roles attract initiative-takers. The gap between them is where projects fail.

Christiaan operated in one of the most regulated environments that exists — oil rigs, safety-critical installations, documented deployment steps, international contracts, no phone support — and held full procedural compliance while still identifying novel problems, deploying unconventional fixes, and escalating when the system was wrong.

The Jordan dead-switch was not a deviation from procedure. The procedure was followed first, exactly. Only when it failed was the initiative applied — and even then, through a methodical diagnostic step, not a guess.

The Azerbaijan contract confrontation was not bypassing hierarchy. It was reading the contract that defined the hierarchy and using it correctly.

The Houston pivot was not ignoring the assignment. It was recognizing the assignment was underusing available capacity and solving for a higher-priority problem.

**Use this in applications to:** Defensie, any government or public sector role, regulated industries (banking, insurance, energy, healthcare), large corporates with compliance requirements. Reframe: "I don't choose between process discipline and initiative — I know when each is needed, and I can apply both."

## WDS Global — The First Blocked Innovation (pre-2012, UK)

### Context

WDS Global was a UK-based outsourced technical support company handling European customer support for multiple telecom operators and phone brands simultaneously — KPN, Orange, T-Mobile, HTC, LG, Nokia, and others. Not a single-client shop; a multi-brand, multi-operator support hub. Christiaan was stationed in Poole, UK, handling Dutch-language support. Within a short time was running the entire Dutch team.

The HTC tutorial video proposal came from working across this multi-brand environment — he could see the same customer education problem repeating across every brand. HTC was the one with the most pressing need: expensive, feature-rich Android devices that customers didn't understand.

### The Video Insight

HTC customers had two options when they didn't understand their device: call the technical hotline (and possibly get routed to an Indian call center with language friction) or read the technical manual (which almost no one did). For an expensive device competing on features, neither option was good enough.

Christiaan's proposal: make online tutorial videos explaining device features, and explore embedding them directly in the device. Help customers understand what they bought, before they get frustrated.

The proposal went to HTC's head office in Japan. They were excited.

### The Blocked Execution

The UK office declined to provide resources. Their response: do it in your free time, with no support.

Christiaan could not do that. The idea was handed to an internal "innovation team" — six people. They came back with the conclusion that a YouTube channel would cost €100,000. The project died.

**What this revealed:** The gap between having a good idea and being able to turn it into a funded proposal was not a creativity problem — it was a business case problem. Christiaan did not have the vocabulary or framework to write a proposal that could command resources. The innovation team did not appear to try seriously at all.

This was the moment that drove him to choose Business IT & Management at HvA. He needed to learn how to turn ideas into proposals.

**Postscript:** HTC eventually did produce tutorial videos independently — but too late. By then they had already lost significant market share to competitors. The idea was correct. The timing mattered. The organization that could have executed it first chose not to.

**Use this framing:** Not "my idea got stolen." Rather: "I saw what needed to happen, the org couldn't move fast enough, and the market proved the point." Classic early-signal problem. Use for roles about innovation velocity, organizational change, or why transformation speed matters.

### Scotland

The UK office did send him to Scotland to train retail staff on how the phones actually worked — how to demonstrate features, explain value, help sales. Fun work. Consistent with what would become a long pattern: teach the people on the ground, not just the product.

### Why He Left

After more than a year with WDS Global — running the Dutch team, proposing ideas that reached Japan, being sent across the country — the company kept him on a temporary contract. Extended three times. Each renewal, one metric on his scorecard was just below the threshold for permanent status. The rest were consistently high.

He recognized the pattern: one metric, always just wrong, regardless of performance. He left.

**Character note:** Early recognition of exploitation. Did not stay hoping the situation would change. Also: the first time he saw a company use bureaucratic process (scorecard management) to avoid honoring what a person had actually contributed.
